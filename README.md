## What is this project?

I built this dashboard to help retail businesses understand their sales data without needing to dig through spreadsheets or wait for end-of-month reports. The idea is simple — put all the important numbers in one place, make them visual, and let anyone in the business answer their own questions.

The dashboard covers sales Discount Mart data form 2016 to 2019 and shows:

- How much was sold each month
- Which product categories made the most money
- Where in the country sales were strongest
- How many items customers typically buy in one order

---

## The problem I was trying to solve

Most small and mid-sized retail businesses have plenty of data, but it sits locked away in their systems. The people who need it — managers, buyers, store teams — often don't have an easy way to look at it. They end up waiting for someone to pull a report, or making decisions based on gut feeling.

I've seen this happen a lot. A business doesn't know December is going to be their biggest month until it's already here and they've run out of stock. Or they keep spending the same amount on every product category, not realising one of them is quietly making far more money than the others.

This dashboard tries to fix that. It gives you the answers before the problem happens.

## What the dashboard shows

### Monthly Sales Trend

This is a line chart that shows sales for every month of the year. You can see at a glance which months were strong and which were slow. There's also a dotted average line so you always know whether any given month is above or below normal.

For example, sales in January(2018) were $18,542 — well below average. By December(2018) they hit $96,999. That kind of pattern tells a business to stock up early in Q4 and run promotions in Q1 to bring those quiet months up.

### Sales by Category

This chart breaks sales down into three categories: Technology , Furniture , and Office Supplies . It's a simple horizontal bar chart but it tells you a lot. Technology is clearly the strongest — so if you're a buyer, you know to push harder there and negotiate better deals with suppliers.

### Sales Map

This is a map of the US showing which states are generating the most revenue. Some states are doing very well (Texas at $41,686) while others barely show up. That tells the marketing team exactly where to focus their next campaign, or where it might be worth opening a new location.

### Order Quantity Chart

This histogram shows how many items people tend to buy in a single order. Most customers buy 2–3 items at a time. A small number buy 10 or more, which suggests they're businesses buying in bulk. Knowing this helps with things like pricing bundles or identifying which customers to treat as B2B accounts.

### The three headline numbers

At the top of the dashboard you can see total sales ($609,206), total profit ($182,762), and total units sold (9,837). These update when you change the year filter. That profit figure is around 30% of sales — useful to know without doing any maths.

## How can other businesses use this?

Anyone who runs a retail or e-commerce business can use something like this. You don't need expensive software. Tableau Public is free.

All you need is a basic export from your sales system with these columns:

- Date of sale
- Revenue
- Profit
- Quantity sold
- Product category
- Location (city or state)

Once you have that, you can connect it to Tableau and build the same charts. It works with data from Shopify, Amazon, WooCommerce, SAP, or even just a well-organised Excel file.

## What difference does it actually make?

Here are some real benefits businesses typically see when they start using a dashboard like this:

- **Faster decisions** — instead of waiting for a weekly report, managers can check the numbers any time
- **Less wasted stock** — when you can see demand patterns clearly, you order the right amount
- **Better marketing spend** — you stop spending equally everywhere and focus on the places and times that actually work
- **Happier teams** — people argue less about "what the data says" when everyone is looking at the same screen
